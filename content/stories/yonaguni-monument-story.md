## How It Started

I first learned about the Yonaguni Monument through Graham Hancock's work. If you've read his books, you know what I'm talking about - he opens your eyes to this idea that we're "a species with amnesia." We've forgotten huge chunks of our own history. Yonaguni was one of those mysteries he wrote about, and it stuck with me.

Back in 1986, a local dive operator named Kihachiro Aratake found something incredible 25 meters underwater off Japan's westernmost island. Massive rock formations with terraces, steps, platforms, and walls that have these crazy sharp edges and right angles. Too geometric to just be random rocks, you know?

Reading about it was one thing. But actually standing on Yonaguni Island, knowing this thing was right beneath those waves - something that could completely change how we think about ancient civilizations - I had to see it myself.

## My First Attempt (That Didn't Happen)

Here's the thing though - my first visit to Yonaguni didn't go as planned. I showed up in summer, standing on the shore watching these massive waves crash. The currents were insane, wind was howling, and the local dive shops just shook their heads. "Not today. Too dangerous."

And honestly? I wasn't even certified yet. So I just stood there, staring at the water, knowing the monument was down there but completely out of reach. Being that close and not being able to dive it - that feeling really got to me.

That's actually one of the main reasons I got my diving license. I needed to come back. I needed to see this thing with my own eyes, not just in books or documentaries.

I learned something important that first trip: you can't just show up at Yonaguni and hope for the best. Planning matters. Timing matters. Summer is terrible - strong winds and currents make it impossible or just plain dangerous. The best time is late autumn through early spring when the sea calms down.

When I finally returned - certified, prepared, and during the right season - the monument was still there waiting. This time, nothing was going to stop me.

## Finally Diving It

Even on a good day, diving the monument isn't easy. The currents are strong, visibility can be rough. But as I descended through the blue water, my heart was pounding - not from fear, but from anticipation. This wasn't just another dive. This was years in the making.

When the structure came into view through the haze, I just stopped and stared. Massive stone terraces stepping down into the depths. Sharp, angular edges. Parallel grooves carved into flat surfaces. What looked like an actual road or pathway. The scale was insane - these weren't just random rocks worn down by water. These looked designed.

Swimming along these walls and platforms, I couldn't shake the feeling I was looking at something people built. The current kept pushing me, but I held on, running my hands along surfaces that felt way too smooth, way too straight to be natural.

## A Species with Amnesia

The official scientific story? Natural formation. Just erosion and tectonic shifts over time. But a lot of researchers disagree. Masaaki Kimura, a marine geologist who's studied this site for decades, believes it shows clear evidence of human work. Some think it could be 10,000 years old or more - from back when sea levels were way lower, before the last ice age ended.

If that's true, it means there was an advanced civilization way earlier than mainstream archaeology is willing to admit. And that's exactly what Graham Hancock has been saying for years - that there were sophisticated cultures in our ancient past, but their achievements got lost. Buried under rising seas, destroyed by catastrophes, forgotten.

We're a species with amnesia. We've forgotten our own story. Places like Yonaguni are the fragments left behind - clues to civilizations that existed and then vanished before what we call "civilization" even started.

Swimming through that place, I felt it. All those lost thousands of years. How much have we forgotten? What knowledge is just gone, swallowed by the ocean? What could our ancestors do that we're only now starting to figure out again?

## Capturing the Moment

I wanted this shot to show the scale, so I waited for another diver to swim into frame - that tiny human figure against these massive stone formations. That's what really hits you when you're down there. How small we are. How enormous this thing is.

The light coming down from the surface creates this otherworldly atmosphere, hitting those geometric edges while shadows hide the deeper parts. That's what drew me to this place - seeing modern humans trying to understand what our ancestors might have built, then lost.

This is why Yonaguni matters. It challenges everything we think we know. History isn't settled. There are mysteries still waiting under the water, buried in the earth, hiding in plain sight. They're asking us to remember what we forgot, to reclaim our real heritage as a species that's way older and maybe way more capable than we've been taught.

The Yonaguni Monument isn't just about an unknown past. It's about the questions we need to keep asking, the mysteries we need to keep exploring, and the amnesia we need to overcome.
